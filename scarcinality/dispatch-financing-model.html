The Dollar Reroute

The prior readings said the money changes address, not amount. This dispatch shows the arithmetic — the national financing model, every line adjustable, every conclusion falsifiable.

A conditional reading through scarcinality, built on Fisher, Keynes, and Minsky. This is a direction, not a forecast, and it is falsifiable. It follows directly from The Counterfeit Shortage of Care and The Order of Operations. Figures from CMS NHE 2024, BEA wages and salaries, Tax Policy Center, and McKinsey and the single-payer literature on administrative savings.

The hardest thing to believe about the financing of universal care is that it requires no new money in any aggregate sense. The national health bill runs about 5.3 trillion dollars. About half of that is already public — Medicare, Medicaid, the VA, and the federal tax subsidy for employer coverage. The private remainder is already being paid, by employers as premiums, by workers as premiums, and by households directly as out-of-pocket costs. The framework's claim is not that the bill disappears. It is that rerouting the money already in motion covers it, and that each payer ends up lighter than before.

This dispatch builds the model in public. Every number comes from a named source, and the three genuinely contested parameters — how much administrative friction a single payer eliminates, what rate an employer payroll contribution is set at, and how much of the tax exclusion Congress recovers — are adjustable. The conclusions that survive across the full range of those parameters are the ones the framework is prepared to defend. The ones that depend on landing the optimistic assumption are noted as such.

Two findings hold across most of the model's range. Employers pay less so long as the payroll rate is set below roughly 9.1 percent — below what they currently pay in premiums — which is every plausible scenario. Households pay less in aggregate so long as administrative savings land above approximately 330 billion dollars, which covers the conservative estimate and everything above it. Below that level, households as an aggregate move from slightly ahead to roughly at parity with today, while still receiving the out-of-pocket cap and the end of medical bankruptcy risk.

The single parameter that can flip the household verdict from better to worse is the administrative savings figure, which is contested but not unconstrained. The existing literature, from the McKinsey floor at 265 billion to the upper academic estimates near 600 billion, reflects genuine uncertainty about realized savings in a system that retains some private-layer complexity versus one that fully centralizes billing. Both columns of that range still leave employers materially better off.

The dollars do not appear. They change address. What the sliders are testing is whether the new address is lighter for each payer than the old one was.

The distributional cut

The national aggregate conceals the distributional question, which is the more interesting one for households. An income-based contribution means asking higher earners to pay more than they paid in flat premiums. The table below shows the illustrative household-level arithmetic at the model's contribution rates. The crossover — above which a household pays more in contributions than it paid in premiums plus out-of-pocket — sits near 168,000 dollars of household income. Below that line, which is most households, the direct payment falls. Above it, the direct payment rises, while the household still receives the out-of-pocket cap, the elimination of medical-bankruptcy risk, and, for the uninsured among them, coverage they did not previously have.

The private-layer trade

The conservative scenario — retaining complementary private insurance and insurers administering the public benefit — preserves roughly a million jobs in insurance administration but gives back about 185 billion dollars of annual savings. Under that arrangement, households in aggregate move from clearly better to roughly at parity. The just-transition cost for the displaced workforce in the central case is estimated near 107 billion dollars, covered several times over by even the conservative savings figure in the first year alone. The trade is less efficiency for meaningfully less disruption. It is a political choice, not a mathematical one. Budget against the conservative column and treat the central case as upside.

The arithmetic is not the argument. The argument is that nothing real has run out. The model shows that the arithmetic holds across a wide range of contested assumptions. What the sliders cannot move is the care that already exists — the clinicians practicing, the hospitals built, the drugs manufactured. As of this date the reading stands. The plane will say when it stops.
